## Research questions

Use FINER as a first check: feasible, interesting, novel, ethical, and relevant. Then specify:

- Population, phenomenon, system, corpus, or setting.
- Intervention/exposure/comparison where applicable.
- Outcome or object of explanation.
- Time and jurisdiction boundaries.
- Unit of analysis.
- Whether the objective is descriptive, comparative, causal, predictive, interpretive, theoretical, or evaluative.

Avoid a research question that promises causal inference from a design that supports association only.

## Design fit

Match the question to defensible evidence:

| Objective | Typical evidence |
|---|---|
| Describe | Survey, observation, corpus, administrative data |
| Compare | Controlled or matched comparison, comparative cases |
| Explain causally | Randomization or explicit identification strategy |
| Predict | Held-out evaluation, temporal/external validation |
| Interpret | Qualitative interviews, texts, observations, reflexive analysis |
| Synthesize | Transparent review protocol and study-level evidence |
| Develop theory | Construct definitions, propositions, boundary conditions |

State sampling, measures, analytic plan, assumptions, limitations, and ethics. Separate planned methods from methods actually performed.

## Literature search

Define:

1. Review question and conceptual facets.
2. Databases, repositories, registries, citation chaining, and grey-literature policy.
3. Search strings with synonyms, controlled vocabulary, and exclusions.
4. Date, language, geography, discipline, and publication-type boundaries.
5. Inclusion/exclusion criteria before screening.
6. Deduplication and screening process.
7. Quality or risk-of-bias assessment appropriate to study type.
8. Search date and reproducible query record.

Use current primary documentation for database syntax. Do not claim a systematic review when search and screening are not systematic.

## Screening and synthesis

Maintain a study matrix containing citation, design, sample/context, measures, methods, findings, limitations, quality, and relevance to each claim.

Synthesize rather than list:

- Group studies by concept, method, mechanism, chronology, or disagreement.
- Explain why findings converge or diverge.
- Distinguish absence of evidence from evidence of absence.
- Weight strong, direct evidence more than convenient or frequently cited evidence.
- Identify gaps only after showing what was searched and found.

## Evidence map

For each major claim record:

| Claim | Evidence | Source status | Reasoning | Counterevidence | Limitation | Destination |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

No major empirical claim should enter a final draft without an evidence-map row or an explicit placeholder.

## Reproducibility

Record source versions, queries, dates, data/code identifiers, transformations, analysis settings, exclusions, and artifact hashes when available. Use preregistration or a timestamped protocol for confirmatory work. Label deviations and exploratory analyses rather than rewriting history.
