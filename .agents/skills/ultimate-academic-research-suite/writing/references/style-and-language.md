## Register and clarity

Prefer precise nouns and verbs, explicit logical relations, and discipline-appropriate terminology. Define contested or specialized terms operationally. Avoid unsupported intensifiers, vague quantifiers, inflated novelty, unnecessary jargon, and ceremonial openings.

Use first person when it clarifies author action and the venue permits it. Do not use passive voice merely to sound academic.

## Voice and tense

Typical patterns:

- Present: established knowledge, paper content, tables/figures.
- Present perfect: a body of prior work continuing to the present.
- Past: completed methods, observations, and specific studies.
- Modal/conditional: uncertainty, implications, and proposed work.

Follow disciplinary and venue conventions where they differ.

## Hedging

Calibrate rather than weaken automatically:

| Evidence strength | Example stance |
|---|---|
| Direct and robust | demonstrates within the studied conditions |
| Consistent but bounded | indicates, supports |
| Plausible interpretation | suggests, may reflect |
| Speculative | could, might, warrants investigation |

Avoid both overclaiming and excessive hedging that obscures a supported conclusion.

## Cohesion

Use repeated key terms deliberately, pronouns with unmistakable antecedents, and transitions that name the relation: contrast, cause, example, consequence, sequence, or qualification. A transition cannot repair a missing logical step.

## Concision

Remove:

- Throat-clearing and meta-commentary.
- Nominalizations where a verb is clearer.
- Double hedges and redundant qualifiers.
- Repeated conclusions.
- Generic claims of importance.
- Long strings of nouns without clear relations.

Retain methodological and ethical detail needed for evaluation.

## Multilingual writing

Follow the target language's scholarly conventions rather than translating English syntax mechanically. Preserve technical terms when translation would introduce ambiguity. Maintain a terminology list for long projects. For bilingual abstracts, verify that both versions report the same objective, methods, results, and conclusion; do not treat one as a loose summary.

Use the English phrasebank only for English prose. Do not present machine-translated phrasebank expressions as canonical language in another language.

## Quality audit

Check:

1. Each paragraph has one defensible function.
2. Claims are specific and evidence-calibrated.
3. Sentence rhythm and paragraph length are not mechanically uniform.
4. Transitions describe real logic.
5. Terms, abbreviations, spelling variety, capitalization, and numerals are consistent.
6. Quotations and paraphrases preserve source meaning.
7. Phrasebank language has been adapted and is not repetitive.
8. Editing has not changed statistical or epistemic meaning.
