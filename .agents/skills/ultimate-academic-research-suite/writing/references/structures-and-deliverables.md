## Structure selection

Use the target venue first. Where no template exists, select the structure that best exposes the question, evidence, contribution, and limitations. Allocate words according to argumentative importance rather than equal section length.

Every outline should state for each section:

- Communicative purpose.
- Main claims.
- Required evidence.
- Link to preceding/following sections.
- Expected tables, figures, or appendices.
- Word budget.

## IMRaD empirical paper

### Introduction

Establish the problem and significance, synthesize the most relevant background, identify the defensible gap, state the objective/question/hypothesis, and preview the contribution. Do not use a broad topic gap when the study answers a narrow question.

### Methods

Enable evaluation and replication: design, setting, participants/data, sampling, variables/materials, procedure, analysis, robustness, ethics, and deviations. Use past tense for completed procedures and distinguish preregistered from exploratory work.

### Results

Report findings in the order of the questions or hypotheses. Give denominators, effect estimates, uncertainty, and missing-data information where relevant. Do not duplicate every table value in prose or interpret beyond the analysis.

### Discussion

Answer the question, interpret findings against prior work, consider mechanisms and alternatives, explain implications, state limitations and boundary conditions, and avoid causal or universal claims unsupported by the design.

### Conclusion

Synthesize the contribution without copying the abstract, adding new evidence, or exceeding demonstrated scope.

## Theoretical or conceptual paper

1. Problem and construct definitions.
2. Scope and assumptions.
3. Existing explanatory limits.
4. New framework, propositions, or mechanism.
5. Comparison with alternatives.
6. Boundary conditions and testable implications.
7. Contribution and limitations.

## Literature review

Choose the correct label:

- Narrative review: interpretive synthesis with transparent scope.
- Scoping review: map concepts, evidence types, and gaps.
- Systematic review: predefined reproducible search, screening, appraisal, and synthesis.
- Meta-analysis: quantitative synthesis with justified effect model and heterogeneity analysis.
- Integrative review: combine diverse evidence through an explicit framework.

Report search and selection methods proportionate to the label.

## Thesis or dissertation

Use institution rules first. A common structure is:

1. Introduction, problem, questions, contribution, and roadmap.
2. Critical literature review and framework.
3. Methodology and methods, including epistemological assumptions where relevant.
4. Results/findings.
5. Discussion.
6. Conclusion, contributions, limitations, and future work.

Maintain a thesis-level argument across chapters. End each chapter by stating what it established and why the next chapter follows.

## Conference paper

Prioritize one contribution under a strict word/page limit. Compress background and secondary analyses before removing methods needed for evaluation. Ensure anonymization where required.

## Policy brief

Lead with the decision problem, audience, key evidence, feasible options, tradeoffs, recommendation, implementation, and uncertainty. Separate evidence from advocacy.

## Abstracts

Draft the abstract after the paper is stable. Include, as appropriate:

- Context/problem.
- Objective or question.
- Design/methods.
- Principal results with quantitative detail when available.
- Conclusion limited to the evidence.
- Contribution or implication.

Follow structured headings when required. Avoid citations, undefined abbreviations, background inflation, and claims absent from the paper. Select searchable, specific keywords not already redundant with the title.
