## Argument blueprint

Represent major arguments as:

1. Claim.
2. Evidence.
3. Warrant explaining why the evidence supports the claim.
4. Qualification defining scope and certainty.
5. Counterevidence or alternative explanation.
6. Response.
7. Implication.

Do not treat citation quantity as argument quality. Prefer direct, methodologically suitable evidence.

## Claim calibration

Match verbs and certainty to evidence:

- Descriptive: reports, shows in this sample, was associated with.
- Comparative: was higher/lower under the measured conditions.
- Predictive: predicted held-out outcomes with stated evaluation.
- Causal: caused or led to only when identification supports causality.
- Interpretive: suggests, indicates, may reflect, participants described.
- Theoretical: proposes, explains, implies, predicts.

Preserve negative and null findings. Do not turn a non-significant result into evidence of no effect without an equivalence or adequately powered test.

## Paragraph construction

Use a functional paragraph:

- Opening claim or controlling idea.
- Evidence and source integration.
- Analysis or reasoning.
- Qualification/counterpoint where needed.
- Link to the section argument.

Vary sentence length and structure naturally. Do not force every paragraph into an identical template.

## Section drafting

Before drafting a section, list its purpose, claims, evidence, and transition. Then:

1. Draft from the evidence map.
2. Insert citations at the point of support.
3. Mark missing details as explicit placeholders.
4. Check terminology and scope.
5. End with the section's contribution to the overall argument.

Never generate results, sample details, quotations, effect sizes, or references to make prose appear complete.

## Counterarguments

Address the strongest relevant alternative, not a weak substitute. State it fairly, identify the evidence or assumption on which it depends, respond proportionately, and concede residual uncertainty. Use limitations to define validity, not as a ritual list.

## Tables and figures

Use a visual only when it communicates relationships or exact comparisons more clearly than prose. Ensure:

- Standalone title/caption and definitions.
- Units, sample size, uncertainty, and notes.
- Consistency with text and source data.
- Colorblind-safe and grayscale-readable design.
- No decorative dimensional effects or truncated axes that mislead.
- Editable or reproducible generation when possible.
